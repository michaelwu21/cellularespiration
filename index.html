What is Cellular Respiration?

Cellular Respiration is a series of chemical reactions that convert the energy in food molecules into ATP
ATP, or adenosine triphosphate is a form of energy that your body uses to really, do anything, from jumping around screaming to performing active transport
(A form of transporting molecules in/out of the cell using energy)


In other words, cellular respiration turns oxygen and glucose, which are the reactants, into carbon dioxide, water, and most important of all, ATP, the products.

But wait, how does the cell exactly convert these food molecules into usable energy?

First, the cell takes the glucose from the food, and with that, comes the first step of cellular respiration.

Glycolysis and what it does:

Glycolysis is the first step in cellular respiration, and where glucose is basically broken down into 2 energy molucules, and also smaller molecules which occurs in the cytoplasm.
Here's the basic formula to glycolysis(below), and as you can see, this first step of cellular respiration does not require oxygen.

These smaller molecules then go into the mitochondria and the next step of cellular respiration begins...

The second step of Cellular Respiration

After the smaller molecules enter the mitochondria of eukaryotic cells, the process of the krebs cycle and electron transport chain starts.

In this step, the smaller molecules left over from glycolysis is converted into large amounts of ATP, with waste materials of water and carbon dioxide.

And that is basically all to it. Overall, cellular respiration can produce a maximum of 38 ATP molecules for prokaryotic while eukaryotic cells can make a maximum of 36 ATP molecules
